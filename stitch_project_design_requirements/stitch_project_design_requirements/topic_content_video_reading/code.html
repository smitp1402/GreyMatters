Section 4.02

DNA Replication: Molecular Machinery

A comprehensive technical review of the semi-conservative replication process, focusing on the enzymatic roles within the eukaryotic replisome.

Lecture 4.2: The Replisome Mechanism

Duration: 18:45 • Dr. Aris Thorne

transcribe download

I. The Initiation complex

DNA replication is a highly orchestrated event requiring the collective action of numerous specialized enzymes. At the heart of this process lies the Helicase enzyme, which functions as a molecular motor to unwind the double helix. Utilizing ATP hydrolysis, helicase breaks the hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs, creating the replication fork.

II. Primer Synthesis and Elongation

Because DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to an existing 3' hydroxyl group, Primase must first synthesize a short RNA primer. This provides the necessary chemical "foothold" for DNA Polymerase III to begin building the new strand in the 5' to 3' direction.

Fig 4.2.A: Schematic of the Replication Fork

Note the orientation of the leading and lagging strands. The discontinuous synthesis of Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand is dictated by the unidirectional nature of polymerase activity.

III. Processing and Ligation

Following elongation, the RNA primers must be removed and replaced with DNA. DNA Polymerase I performs this exonuclease activity, while DNA Ligase catalyzes the formation of phosphodiester bonds, effectively "stitching" the fragments together into a continuous double helix.

Key Terminology

  • Topoisomerase Relieves torsional strain ahead of the replication fork.
  • SSB Proteins Stabilize single-stranded DNA to prevent re-annealing.
  • Okazaki Fragments Discontinuous DNA segments synthesized on the lagging strand.
